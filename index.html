Research Interests

I'm interested in a question that sits at the intersection of NLP, translation studies, and cultural understanding: can the cultural mediation skill that makes literary translators great — the judgment calls about adaptation, the audience awareness, the instinct for when to preserve foreignness and when to domesticate — be decomposed into learnable components and taught to a model through reinforcement learning?

This question comes from years of being embedded in the Japanese-to-English fan translation community for anime, manga, and visual novels. The translation decisions that matter most in that space aren't linguistic — they're cultural. When keigo encodes a shifting power dynamic, what should the English reader get instead? When a light novel's comedy depends on tsukkomi/boke timing that doesn't map onto English, what do you sacrifice? When mono no aware shows up through seasonal imagery, how much of that can land for someone reading in translation?

Current MT systems produce output that's often linguistically competent but culturally flat. I believe this cultural mediation layer is separable from bilingual linguistic ability and trainable on its own — through preference learning from people with cultural expertise rather than language expertise. The model supplies the linguistic competence; human cultural judgment teaches it everything else.

The fiction I care about most pushes this even further: works where language itself is the art. The Monogatari series builds scenes around Japanese wordplay that English can only gesture at. Joyce makes English do things it wasn't designed to do. Visual novels use language as mechanic — puns as plot points, kanji readings with double meanings. In these works, culture and language are so entangled that you can't address one without the other. That's the hardest version of this problem, and the version I want to work on.

Japanese-English is my starting point because it's what I know from the inside. But the framework is language-agnostic. Longer term, I want to build translation systems with enough cultural and linguistic intelligence to help people discover great fiction from traditions they'd otherwise never encounter — and eventually connect that to cross-cultural discovery across creative mediums: literature, music, film, and beyond.

Current Projects

Cultural Adaptation Error Analysis for Japanese-English Literary MT

Comparing professional human translations of Japanese fiction against outputs from GPT-4, Claude, and TranslateGemma. Building a taxonomy of cultural adaptation failure modes — reference flattening, register collapse, pragmatic mistranslation, tonal drift — and examining whether these errors are systematic, predictable by passage type, and invisible to standard automatic metrics (COMET, MetricX, BERTScore).

literary MT evaluation cultural adaptation in progress

github.com/weidili/cultural-mt-analysis

Cross-Cultural Aesthetic Mapping in Music

Using Spotify's public API to analyze how mood and genre descriptors (e.g., "nostalgic," "melancholic," "energetic") correspond to different acoustic profiles across Japanese, American, and Brazilian listening contexts. Testing whether categories that recommendation systems treat as universal are actually culturally particular.
